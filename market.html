Part 1

Stedi in plain English

A clearinghouse is the switchboard between a biller and the insurance payers. Stedi is the modern, developer-first version of that switchboard.

What it is

A programmable clearinghouse

The pipe that carries eligibility checks, claims, and remittances between you and payers, exposed as a clean API instead of a legacy file drop.

The clever part

JSON over X12

Insurance runs on X12 EDI: cryptic, fixed-format files (270/271 eligibility, 837 claim, 835 remit). Stedi lets you send and receive plain JSON and does the ugly X12 translation with each payer for you. That removes the single hardest part of building billing software.

How they charge

Transparent per transaction

Published usage pricing, roughly a few cents to a few dimes per transaction, no monthly minimum. Self-serve signup, free sandbox, SOC 2 and HIPAA-eligible with a BAA.

The catch

Young, and just a pipe

Only about two years as a clearinghouse (they pivoted during the 2024 Change Healthcare outage). And they are transport only: they move the data, they do not do the billing work.

Part 4

How we would price it

A standalone, AI-native platform. The trick: because AI does the work, we can price on outcomes and volume, not on human seats.

Practice
$1.5 to 3k / mo
  • Small specialty group or practice
  • Platform + AI prefill and scrub
  • Per-claim fee on top
Group (most common)
$5 to 15k / mo
  • Multi-provider or billing company
  • Full platform + Remit module
  • Per-claim + transaction pass-through
Enterprise
$25k+ / mo
  • Large hospital-based groups
  • All modules incl. Arbitron
  • Volume pricing + recovery share
Per claim

$0.75 to $2.50

Per claim worked, versus a pure pipe at a few cents. We charge more because we do the workflow, the AI, and the scrub, not just transport.

AI service

Priced per claim completed

Instead of per human seat, an "AI biller" price per claim the AI finishes. As the work automates, this becomes the core meter.

Modules

Remit + Arbitron add-ons

Auto-reconcile the 835, and recover underpayments through IDR and arbitration. Each attaches to the base platform.

The wedge that changes everything: recovery share

On the out-of-network dollars we recover that the client was not getting before (through IDR, arbitration, and underpayment catch), take 12 to 25% of the incremental recovery. It aligns us with the client, it is high margin, and it is money that did not exist for them without us. This is the line item that makes the platform special rather than just cheaper software.

Valuation framing

What that could be worth

AI-native health-IT SaaS tends to trade around 6 to 12 times ARR when it is growing fast. At $25M ARR that is roughly $150 to 350M; at $70M ARR growing well, it is in the $0.5 to 1B range. For reference, Waystar is public and multi-billion, Stedi raised $142M on about two years as a clearinghouse, and Tennr is around a $600M valuation.

The honest read

Why we could win, and what is hard

Why we could win

  • OON and IDR recovery is a real, NSA-created market the generalists ignore
  • We own the full cycle: billing, remit, and arbitration, not one slice
  • AI-native from day one lowers the cost to serve and lifts margin
  • We prove it on real volume at USMON before selling a line of it
  • Deep specialty knowledge (IONM, hospital-based) is a moat generalists cannot fake

What is hard (the honest part)

  • Payer connectivity and clearinghouse plumbing is genuinely difficult
  • Healthcare sales cycles are long and trust-heavy
  • Compliance and BAA surface grows the moment we go standalone
  • Incumbents (Waystar, Optum) are entrenched and well funded
  • Recovery-share pricing needs clean attribution to defend
